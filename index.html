Who Are We?

Our Mission

Through accessible information, interactive tools, and innovative smart shelter technology, MIAO empowers communities to understand, protect, and care for stray feline populations. By combining compassion with data-driven solutions, we aim to build a more humane and connected urban environment—for cats and for us all.

What affects the distribution of stray cats in cities?

Disclaimer: Due to limitations in data availability and previous studies, this map is a VERY rough estimate of the real distribution of cats, and should be used with caution.

According to studies by Kingsley1, McDonald and Skillings2, Flockhart et. al3, and Hou et. al4, the distribution of stray cats is affected by factors such as human population density, median income, IMD (Index of Multiple Deprivation5), type of land use etc.

Based on these studies and available data for Shanghai, we determined 6 factors affecting the population of stray cats6:

  • Population Density – The more humans living in a given region, the greater the cat population.
  • Housing Density – The more buildings in a region, the greater the cat population.
  • Type of Housing – The more residential area in a region, the greater the cat population.
  • GDP per Capita – The higher the GDP per capita/income, the lower the cat population.
  • Education – The number of schools, percentage of population enrolled, and number of teachers is evaluated to determine the availability of educational resources; the better the education, the fewer stray cats.
  • Healthcare – The number of hospitals, number of hospital beds, and number of medical personnel is evaluated to determine the availability of healthcare resources; the better the healthcare, the fewer stray cats.

To evaluate these factors, normalized values (resources available per person or per unit of land) were calculated for each metric and ranked. These rankings were used to calculate a score for each district, which would again be ranked to indicate which districts are likely to have higher densities of stray cats. The weight of each factor can be adjusted by the user based on what they believe is more important.

Data Sources

  1. Giles Kingsley, DISTRIBUTION AND CORRELATES OF FERAL CAT TRAPPING PERMITS IN LOS ANGELES, CALIFORNIA, December 2015.
  2. Jennifer L. McDonald and Elizabeth Skillings, “Human Influences Shape the First Spatially Explicit National Estimate of Urban Unowned Cat Abundance,” Scientific Reports 11, no. 1 (October 28, 2021), https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-99298-6.
  3. D. T. Flockhart, D. R. Norris, and J. B. Coe, “Predicting Free‐roaming Cat Population Densities in Urban Areas,” Animal Conservation 19, no. 5 (March 1, 2016): 472–83, https://doi.org/10.1111/acv.12264.
  4. Yingxu Hou et al., “合肥市流浪动物活动规律的初步观察,” 野生动物学报, no. 6 (December 3, 2009), https://doi.org/10.19711/j.cnki.issn2310-1490.2009.06.011.
  5. “Broad themes [for measuring IMD] include income, employment, education, health, crime, barriers to housing and services, and the living environment.”
  6. Health care and education are included as indicators of IMD.
